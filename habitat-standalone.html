Where it lives

A life

The platypus is endemic to eastern Australia — found only where freshwater meets dense riverbank vegetation. It is a semi-aquatic specialist: a creature of margins, burrows, and cold, clean streams.

Range: Queensland → Victoria → Tasmania · Elevation: sea level to 1,000 m

01 Zone One

Queensland &
Subtropical Streams

From the Atherton Tablelands south through the coastal ranges, platypuses inhabit the cool, shaded waterways fed by Queensland's highland rainforests. The Daintree catchment and Carnarvon Gorge represent the northern and western edges of their continental range. Here, dense riparian vegetation provides ideal burrowing sites along steep clay banks.

WaterwaysMossman, Mary, Noosa, Burnett rivers
Elevation50 – 800 m above sea level
Key featureSubtropical rainforest canopy shading
ThreatDrought, agricultural runoff, sedimentation

02 Zone Two

NSW & Victoria —
The Heartland

The Murray-Darling basin, the rivers of the Great Dividing Range, and the cool streams of the Victorian highlands form the core of platypus territory. Healesville, Tidbinbilla, and the upper Murrumbidgee are among the most reliably sighted locations on the continent. Cool, clear water with abundant invertebrate prey — shrimp, yabbies, insect larvae — defines the ideal conditions.

WaterwaysMurrumbidgee, Yarra, Snowy, Lachlan
ElevationSea level – 1,000 m
Key featureHighest documented population density
ThreatRiver regulation, water extraction

03 Zone Three

Tasmania —
Cold & Pristine

Tasmania harbours some of the most intact platypus habitat on Earth. The island's relatively undisturbed river systems — including the Arthur, Huon, and Derwent — run cold and clean through temperate rainforest. Tasmanian platypuses tend to be larger than their mainland counterparts, an adaptation to the colder climate, and populations here are considered among the most stable.

WaterwaysHuon, Derwent, Arthur, Mersey rivers
ElevationSea level – 900 m
Key featureLargest body size; most stable populations
ThreatIntroduced predators (foxes); climate change

A habitat
under
pressure.

IUCN Near Threatened · 2016
Climate change & drought

Reduced rainfall and prolonged drought events lower stream flows, concentrating platypuses in smaller refuges and increasing competition and predation risk.

Land clearing & sedimentation

Agriculture and development strip riparian vegetation, destabilising banks used for burrows and increasing sediment loads that smother invertebrate prey.

Water extraction & dams

River regulation alters flow regimes and water temperatures, fragmenting populations and reducing the cold, well-oxygenated conditions platypuses require.

Entanglement & pollution

Discarded fishing line, yabby traps, and plastic debris cause drowning; agricultural and urban runoff degrades water quality and depletes food sources.
